# Spandrels in Biology and Design: Rethinking Purpose in What We Create

In both biology and product design, not everything that exists is the result of careful, intentional engineering. Some features arise simply because they have to. Understanding _why_ can sharpen how we think, how we design, and how we explain the products and systems around us.

One of the most useful concepts for challenging the assumption of purposeful design is the idea of the **spandrel**.

## What Is a Spandrel?

**In architecture**, a spandrel is the triangular space that forms as a necessary byproduct between two arches. The space itself isn’t designed for a function—it just happens. Later, architects might decorate these spaces, but their existence is an outcome of structural constraints, not purpose.

**In biology**, paleontologist Stephen Jay Gould and geneticist Richard Lewontin borrowed the term to challenge the belief that every trait in an organism must be adaptive. They argued that many biological features are like spandrels: side-effects of other functional traits, not selected for directly.

### Example: The Human Chin

Humans are the only primates with chins. Some might argue this feature must provide an evolutionary advantage—perhaps aiding speech or social signaling. But the spandrel argument suggests otherwise. The chin may simply result from changes in facial structure—such as a shortened face and repositioned jaw—making the chin a structural consequence, not an adaptation.

## Why This Matters in Design

The concept of spandrels isn’t just a critique of evolutionary theory. It offers a powerful lens for thinking about **product design**.

We often assume that every feature in a product has a purpose. But just like in nature, many features arise not from intent but as **byproducts of other decisions**.

### 1. Unintended By-products of Design Choices

When you optimize for one attribute, others emerge as structural consequences.

- Choosing a durable material might make the product heavier or harder to recycle.
    
- Designing software for scalability might make it more complex and harder to maintain.
    
- Using a manufacturing process that reduces cost might constrain the aesthetic or ergonomics.
    

These are not "designed features" in the traditional sense—they're architectural consequences of other choices.

### 2. Avoiding the Just-So Story

In evolutionary biology, a "just-so story" is a plausible but untested explanation for why a trait exists. Designers do this too—rationalizing unintended features post hoc, instead of critically evaluating whether they are necessary or even useful.

Being honest about the origin of features allows for clearer, more purposeful design decisions.

### 3. Tinkerer vs. Inventor Mindset

Gould and Lewontin described evolution as a "tinkerer"—working with what already exists rather than designing from scratch. The same is true in design. Constraints, legacy systems, and user expectations often force us to reuse or recombine existing elements.

This tinkering naturally produces spandrels: elements that exist because they were the most efficient or convenient way to get something else to work.

Recognizing this helps:

- Avoid attributing false intentionality to features
    
- Make better decisions about what to keep or discard
    
- Appreciate creative opportunities in unintended outcomes
    

## Designing with Spandrels in Mind

To apply this thinking in practice:

- **Audit your product**: What features are truly functional? Which ones are inherited, incidental, or structural side-effects?
    
- **Be skeptical of explanations**: Are you inventing reasons to justify something that doesn’t actually help the user?
    
- **Use spandrels creatively**: Sometimes, unintended by-products can be reframed as opportunities—for decoration, interaction, or new uses.
    

## Conclusion

Not everything in design is intentional. Not everything in biology is adaptive. Some things just _are_—the result of constraints, legacy, or structural necessity.

The concept of spandrels invites us to design more critically, to explain more honestly, and to leave space for the unexpected. Good design isn’t about pretending every detail has a purpose. It’s about knowing when to question, when to adapt, and when to embrace the accidental.
